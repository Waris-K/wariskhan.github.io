Understanding the Dirac Delta Function

As someone passionate about physics and its mathematical underpinnings, I’ve always found it fascinating how certain concepts, though abstract, become essential in solving real-world problems. One such concept that has intrigued me for years is the Dirac delta function. I’ve often wondered why such a peculiar function is needed, what role it plays, and how it connects to the physical world around us. It wasn’t until I started diving deeper into the relationships between vector fields and their properties—especially divergence—that I began to fully appreciate the power and elegance of the Dirac delta function.

In this post, I’ll share my journey of understanding the Dirac delta function, the motivation behind using divergence, and how we can apply these concepts in various fields like electromagnetism, gravitational physics, and beyond.

What is the Dirac Delta Function?

At first glance, the Dirac delta function seems almost paradoxical. It's not a function in the traditional sense. Instead, it's what’s called a generalized function or a distribution. Mathematically, the Dirac delta function \(\delta(x)\) is defined as being zero everywhere except at a single point, where it is "infinitely" large in such a way that its integral over all space equals 1:

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(x) \, dx = 1. \]

This peculiar behavior makes it a powerful tool for modeling phenomena that are inherently localized in space or time. To understand its significance, we first need to explore the concept of divergence and why it matters.

Divergence: What Is It and Why Does It Matter?

The concept of divergence is fundamental to understanding vector fields and their behavior in space. Mathematically, divergence measures how much a vector field spreads out or converges at a given point. To lay the groundwork, let’s first define the divergence theorem.

Divergence Theorem

The divergence theorem, also known as Gauss's theorem, relates the flow (flux) of a vector field through a closed surface to the divergence of the field inside the volume enclosed by that surface. In mathematical terms:

\[ \int_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}) \, d\tau = \int_S (\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{n}) \, da \]

Here:

  • \(V\): The volume enclosed by the surface \(S\).
  • \(S\): The closed surface bounding \(V\).
  • \(\mathbf{n}\): The outward unit normal to the surface \(S\).

The divergence theorem can be expressed in various coordinate systems:

  • Cartesian Coordinates (x,y,z):
    • \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial z} \]
  • Cylindrical Coordinates (r, \(\theta\), z):
    • \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r v_r) + \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial v_\theta}{\partial \theta} + \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial z} \]
  • Spherical Coordinates (r, \(\theta\), \(\phi\)):
    • \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r^2 v_r) + \frac{1}{r \sin\theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(\sin\theta v_\theta) + \frac{1}{r \sin\theta} \frac{\partial v_\phi}{\partial \phi} \]

Connecting Divergence to Real-World Fields

To illustrate the concept of divergence and its deeper implications, let’s consider the vector field:

\[ \mathbf{v} = \frac{\hat{r}}{r^2}. \]

This field is directed radially outward at every point in space, with a characteristic \(1/r^2\) dependence. This form is not chosen arbitrarily—it represents:

  • The electric field of a point charge in electrodynamics:
    • \[ \mathbf{E} = \frac{q}{4\pi \epsilon_0 r^2} \hat{r}. \]
  • The gravitational field of a point mass:
    • \[ \mathbf{g} = -\frac{GM}{r^2} \hat{r}. \]
  • Radiation intensity from a point source:
    • \[ I(r) = \frac{P}{4\pi r^2}\hat{r}. \]

Thus, studying this field gives insights applicable to many physical phenomena. But, as we calculate its divergence, we encounter a paradox\(\dots\)

Let us carefully analyze the divergence of the vector field:

\[ \mathbf{v} = \frac{\hat{r}}{r^2}, \]

where \(\hat{r}\) is the radial unit vector and \(r = |\mathbf{r}|\). This field is directed radially outward and decreases in magnitude as \(1/r^2\). Intuitively, such a field appears to have a large divergence. However, as we calculate, an intriguing paradox arises.

Step 1: Calculating Divergence

Using the formula for divergence in spherical coordinates, where \(\mathbf{v} = v_r \hat{r}\) and \(v_r = 1/r^2\), we have:

\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r^2 v_r \right). \]

Substituting \(v_r = \frac{1}{r^2}\):

\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r^2 \cdot \frac{1}{r^2} \right). \]

Simplify the term inside the derivative:

\[ r^2 \cdot \frac{1}{r^2} = 1. \]

Thus:

\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{r^2} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(1) = \frac{1}{r^2} \cdot 0 = 0. \]

This result indicates that the divergence is zero.

Step 2: Calculating the Flux

To further investigate, let’s calculate the flux of \(\mathbf{v}\) through a spherical surface of radius \(R\) centered at the origin. The flux through a closed surface \(S\) is given by:

\[ \Phi = \int_S \mathbf{v} \cdot d\mathbf{a}. \]

In spherical coordinates, the surface area element is:

\[ d\mathbf{a} = R^2 \sin\theta \, d\theta \, d\phi \, \hat{r}. \]

Substituting \(\mathbf{v} = \frac{\hat{r}}{r^2}\) and \(r = R\):

\[ \mathbf{v} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = \frac{\hat{r}}{R^2} \cdot \left( R^2 \sin\theta \, d\theta \, d\phi \, \hat{r} \right) = \sin\theta \, d\theta \, d\phi. \]

Integrating over the entire surface of the sphere:

\[ \Phi = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^\pi \sin\theta \, d\theta \, d\phi. \]

First, evaluate the \(\theta\)-integral:

\[ \int_0^\pi \sin\theta \, d\theta = \left[ -\cos\theta \right]_0^\pi = -\cos\pi + \cos 0 = 2. \]

Next, evaluate the \(\phi\)-integral:

\[ \int_0^{2\pi} d\phi = 2\pi. \]

Combining these results:

\[ \Phi = 2 \cdot 2\pi = 4\pi. \]

The flux through the spherical surface is \(4\pi\), and crucially, this result is independent of the radius \(R\).

Step 3: The Paradox

The flux calculation shows that the total flux is constant at \(4\pi\), no matter how small the sphere. For the divergence theorem to hold, the divergence \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\) must integrate to \(4\pi\) over the volume enclosed by the sphere. However, our divergence calculation showed that \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0\). Is the divergence theorem wrong?

This contradiction is resolved by recognizing that \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\) is not a conventional function but instead a generalized function that captures the behavior of a "point source" at \(r = 0\). This behavior is described using the Dirac delta function which is explained in subsequent sections

The Dirac Delta Function: One and Three Dimensions

The Dirac delta function, \(\delta(x)\), is a generalized function defined as zero everywhere except at a single point, where it becomes infinitely large while maintaining a unit integral.

The One-Dimensional Dirac Delta Function

The one-dimensional Dirac delta function, denoted as \(\delta(x)\), can be visualized as an infinitely tall, infinitesimally narrow spike centered at \(x = 0\), with an area of 1 (see Figure 2). Mathematically:

\[ \delta(x) = \begin{cases} 0, & x \neq 0, \\ \infty, & x = 0, \end{cases} \] \[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty \delta(x) \, dx = 1. \]

Although \(\delta(x)\) is not a function in the traditional sense—since its value is infinite at \(x = 0\)—it is a generalized function or distribution. It can be understood as the limiting case of sequences of ordinary functions, such as rectangles of height \(n\) and width \(1/n\), or isosceles triangles with height \(n\) and base \(2/n\) (see Figure 3).

Properties of the Dirac Delta Function

The delta function has several essential properties, the most important being its ability to "pick out" the value of a function at a specific point. If \(f(x)\) is an ordinary, continuous function, then:

\[ f(x) \delta(x) = f(0) \delta(x), \] \[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x) \delta(x) \, dx = f(0). \]

This means that under an integral, the delta function acts as a sampling operator, extracting the value of \(f(x)\) at \(x = 0\).

The delta function can also be shifted to a different point, \(x = a\), as:

\[ \delta(x - a) = \begin{cases} 0, & x \neq a, \\ \infty, & x = a, \end{cases} \] \[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x) \delta(x - a) \, dx = f(a). \]

The Three-Dimensional Dirac Delta Function

Extending to three dimensions, the Dirac delta function is defined as:

\[ \delta^3(\mathbf{r}) = \delta(x) \delta(y) \delta(z), \]

where \(\mathbf{r} = x\hat{x} + y\hat{y} + z\hat{z}\) is the position vector. The three-dimensional delta function is zero everywhere except at \((x, y, z) = (0, 0, 0)\), and its volume integral is 1:

\[ \int_{\text{all space}} \delta^3(\mathbf{r}) \, d\tau = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty \delta(x) \delta(y) \delta(z) \, dx \, dy \, dz = 1. \]

As in the one-dimensional case, the three-dimensional delta function “picks out” the value of a function at a specific point:

\[ \int_{\text{all space}} f(\mathbf{r}) \delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{a}) \, d\tau = f(\mathbf{a}). \]

Resolution of the Paradox

Returning to our earlier example of \(\mathbf{v} = \frac{\hat{r}}{r^2}\), we now recognize that its divergence can be expressed using the three-dimensional Dirac delta function. While the divergence \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}\) is zero everywhere except at \(r = 0\), its integral over any volume containing the origin is \(4\pi\). This is precisely the defining property of the Dirac delta function:

\[ \nabla \cdot \left( \frac{\hat{r}}{r^2} \right) = 4\pi \delta^3(\mathbf{r}). \]

This compact expression resolves the paradox by accounting for the singularity at the origin. The delta function captures the contribution of the point source at \(r = 0\), ensuring consistency with the divergence theorem.
